Multiple Contact Point Vise

Apr 22, 2012

I spent enough time working with irregularly-shaped parts to be frustrated with traditional flat plate vise designs. This design works very well when grabbing onto parts with two parallel sides, but when grabbing on to parts of any other shape, deformation of the part is required in order to create enough contact surface area for the vise to get a strong hold on the part. When working with plastic parts, deforming the part is often not desired, so I came up with an alternative vise design.

My vise design was inspired by the theory of integration. In calculus, one use of integration is to calculate the are under a curve. If done manually, small rectangles or trapezoids (depending on the method used) are drawn along the curve, and the total area of all of these shapes is equal to a rough estimate of the area under the curve. The accuracy of this calculation is dependent upon the number of sections that the area is segmented into.

The traditional flat plate vise design offers only one section, which is a great 'estimate' if the part one is attempting to grab is square in shape. For irregular shapes, the single section offers an inaccurate estimate, so I sought out a design that would allow a greater number of sections, or contact points.

Enter, the use of an array of individually spring-loaded pins.
